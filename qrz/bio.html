01  ·  The Op

Hi, I'm Eric, W4EWB, operating from Louisville, Kentucky (grid EM78). The call is easy to remember: Eggs, Waffles and Bacon.

I worked at RadioShack through high school in the early 2000s, so earning my General Class license felt a little overdue. I made it about halfway through a computer science and engineering program at the University of Kentucky before realizing I liked people more than machines and changing course to become a psychotherapist. Ham radio has turned out to be a pretty good combination of both worlds.

I'm a father of two girls (with a boy on the way this August!), a psychotherapist by day, and a tinkerer and programmer whenever I can find the time. I enjoy wiring up the shack, experimenting with digital modes, receiving pictures over the air, and building little programs that make the station do something useful. My wife and I also enjoy getting outside and visiting family in Utah whenever we can.

02  ·  The Station
THE CORNER SHACK — FT-710, MINI PC AND MAC MINI
THE WIRE IN THE TREES OUT BACK — SIMPLE, QUIET AND IT GETS OUT

Rig: Yaesu FT-710.   Antenna: Buckmaster DX-OCF 7-band off-center-fed dipole, strung between a maple and a pine in the backyard.   Modes: I spend a lot of time exploring digital modes, especially VarAC, FT8 and SSTV. I enjoy keyboard-to-keyboard contacts on VarAC, chasing a little DX on FT8, and receiving and sending pictures with SSTV. I also enjoy voice from time to time and will occasionally hop on a net. I'm still fairly new to amateur radio, so thanks for your patience if I'm getting the hang of something. A new contact or grid still makes my day.

03  ·  What I Build

Half the fun for me is figuring out what I can build around the radio. A few things I've put together for the W4EWB station:

  • A station control center — one dashboard that helps manage logging, watches the bands and keeps an eye on what's running in the shack.
  • A HamClock-style wall display — a live world map with grayline, space weather, the sun and moon, DX spots and beacons, running on a spare monitor. It's my own evolving, browser-based take on the original HamClock — have a look:
HamClock ▸ Wall Display Source on GitHub ▸
A tribute to the original HamClock by Elwood Downey, WB0OEW (SK) — the shack display he built and gave to the amateur radio community for free. This is an evolving, web-based continuation of his idea.
  • Auto-publishing stations — my SSTV receiver and FT8 monitor post what they hear to the web automatically (have a look below).
  • A few experiments — including letting an AI assistant lend a hand around the shack. Ham radio has always involved tinkering, and this is my version of it.
